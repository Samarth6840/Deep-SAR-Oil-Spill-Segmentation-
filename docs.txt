Full Project Explanation
Now let me walk you through the entire project from beginning to end, the way you would explain it in a viva or write it in a report.

What problem are we solving?
The starting point is a real-world environmental problem. When oil spills happen in the ocean, they need to be detected quickly so that response teams can act. Satellites carry sensors called Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) that take pictures of the ocean from space. SAR is particularly useful because unlike regular cameras, it works through clouds and at night — which makes it ideal for ocean monitoring anywhere on Earth at any time.
The challenge is that reading these SAR images manually requires expert knowledge, takes time, and cannot scale when you need to monitor thousands of square kilometres of ocean continuously. So the question becomes: can we train a computer to automatically look at a SAR image and highlight exactly where the oil spill is? That is precisely what this project does.

What kind of task is this?
This is a semantic segmentation task, which is one of the most detailed things you can ask a computer vision model to do. To understand why, think of it in three levels of difficulty. Classification simply answers "is there an oil spill in this image, yes or no?" Detection goes further and draws a rectangle around where the spill roughly is. Segmentation goes all the way and labels every single pixel in the image — this pixel is oil, this pixel is ocean — producing a precise outline of the spill. That pixel-level precision is what makes segmentation both harder and more useful for real applications.

What dataset did we use?
We used the Deep SAR Oil Spill Segmentation (Refined) dataset from Kaggle. It contains real SAR satellite images from two sensors — PALSAR and Sentinel — paired with hand-drawn binary masks. Each mask is a black and white image of exactly the same size as the SAR image, where white pixels mark the oil spill region and black pixels mark the background ocean. These masks were drawn by human experts who looked at the original images and carefully traced the oil spill boundaries. So the dataset gives the model a perfect "answer sheet" to learn from.
The dataset came with only a small number of images split into train and val folders. To make the dataset large enough for a neural network to learn from meaningfully, we applied data augmentation — randomly flipping images horizontally, flipping them vertically, and rotating them by 90, 180, or 270 degrees. This does not create new information, but it forces the model to learn that an oil spill looks the same regardless of which direction you look at it, which makes the model much more robust. After augmentation, we had over 5000 training samples from the original small set.

What model did we build and why?
We used a U-Net, which is a specific type of convolutional neural network originally designed in 2015 for medical image segmentation. The name comes from its shape — if you draw the architecture, it looks like the letter U.
The left side of the U is called the encoder. It works like a funnel — it takes your 256×256 image and progressively applies convolution layers to extract features (edges, textures, shapes) while reducing the spatial size at each step through max-pooling. Think of it like zooming out to understand the big picture. By the bottom of the encoder, the model has a compact, rich understanding of what is in the image, but it has lost the precise spatial locations.
The right side of the U is the decoder. It does the reverse — it takes that compact representation and progressively upsamples it back to the original 256×256 size, reconstructing the spatial detail. Each upsampling step is paired with a skip connection, which takes the feature map from the corresponding encoder level and concatenates it directly into the decoder. This is the most important innovation in U-Net — it allows the decoder to recover fine spatial details that were lost during downsampling, which is essential for drawing accurate segmentation boundaries.
The final layer produces a single output image of size 256×256 where each pixel has a value between 0 and 1, representing the model's confidence that pixel is oil spill. Applying a threshold of 0.5 converts this into a binary mask.

How did we train it?
Training is the process of adjusting the model's millions of internal parameters so that its predictions get closer and closer to the true masks. We used the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0001, which controls how big each adjustment step is — too large and the model overshoots, too small and it learns too slowly.
For the loss function we used binary cross-entropy, which measures how wrong the model's prediction is for every pixel. The model tries to minimise this loss over repeated passes through the training data. Each full pass through the training set is called an epoch, and we trained for 20 epochs.
An important issue we encountered early on was overfitting — when we had only 5 images, the model simply memorised them rather than learning general features. You could see this clearly in the training curves: training loss kept going down while validation loss stayed flat, and validation accuracy was stuck at 50%, which is no better than random guessing. Adding augmentation to expand the dataset to 5000+ samples solved this.

How do we evaluate it?
We evaluated the model on a held-out test set that was never seen during training. We used three measures. Pixel accuracy sounds impressive at 90%, but it is misleading because most pixels in these images are background ocean — a model that predicts "background" for everything would also score around 90% without learning anything useful at all.
The meaningful metrics are IoU (Intersection over Union) and Dice coefficient. IoU measures the overlap between the predicted oil spill region and the true oil spill region divided by their combined area — think of it as what fraction of the total oil-related area did you get right. Dice is mathematically similar but slightly more generous on small regions. Our model achieved an IoU of 0.5778 and a Dice of 0.6944, which means the model's predicted oil spill shapes overlap meaningfully with the true shapes — genuine learning happened, not just guessing.
We also computed the ROC-AUC curve, which plots the trade-off between catching real oil spills (true positive rate) and falsely flagging clean ocean as oil (false positive rate) across every possible threshold. An AUC close to 1 means the model separates the two classes cleanly. A random model would score 0.5. Our model's AUC above 0.5 confirms it learned a real signal from the data.

What does the Streamlit app do?
The Streamlit app is the deployment layer — it takes your trained model and makes it usable by anyone without writing code. A user can either upload any SAR image or pick directly from the validation set. The app preprocesses the image exactly the same way as training (resize to 256×256, normalise to 0-1), runs it through the loaded model, and displays the original image, the predicted mask, and a red overlay side by side. It also shows the oil spill coverage percentage, confidence score, and the IoU and Dice scores when a validation image is used (since we have the ground truth mask for those). This makes the project feel complete and professional — you go from raw satellite data all the way to a live interactive tool.
